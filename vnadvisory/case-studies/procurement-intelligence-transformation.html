The Situation

$2.4B in Annual Spend, Zero Group-Level Visibility

A large GCC-based trading and distribution conglomerate with $2.4B in annual procurement spend across 14 business units had grown through acquisition over 15 years. Each business unit maintained its own procurement team, its own supplier relationships, and its own systems. There was no group-level spend visibility, no consolidated supplier database, and no common procurement process. The 14 units operated as 14 independent buying organisations with no mechanism for consolidating volume, sharing market intelligence, or monitoring shared supplier risk.

The company's leadership understood that fragmentation was leaving value on the table. The prevailing assumption was that this was an inefficiency problem — one that could be addressed over time through gradual standardisation. A systematic analysis revealed it was also a risk problem of more immediate consequence, and that the two problems were compounding each other in ways that were not visible without consolidated data.

Analysis of 24 months of accounts payable data revealed the full scale of the gap. The same supplier was being managed by four different business units at four different price points for the same product category. Contract compliance in indirect spend categories was estimated at below 40% — 62% of spend in MRO, services, logistics, and IT was flowing outside negotiated contracts to unmanaged spot purchases at above-contract pricing. And three significant supply disruptions in the prior 24 months had resulted in emergency procurement at above-market cost, all from suppliers whose financial distress signals had been visible for months before the disruption materialised.

  • Category managers were spending an estimated 70% of their time on purchase order processing, supplier communication, and data reconciliation — less than 10% of procurement team time was available for strategic supplier development or market intelligence.
  • Seven different procurement systems were running across the group, none integrated with the others, making consolidated reporting impossible and cross-unit supplier analysis entirely manual.
  • The absence of group-level visibility meant that volume leverage — the most reliable mechanism for procurement savings at scale — was being systematically unused across every major spend category.
Our Approach

Four-Pillar Transformation Over Nine Months

VN Advisory structured the transformation around four parallel workstreams — spend intelligence, supplier intelligence, category management restructure, and technology consolidation — sequenced so that each produced usable results independently while building toward the integrated capability that made the full savings programme possible.

Pillar 1: Spend Intelligence Platform

A group-level spend analytics platform was deployed with data feeds from all 14 business unit ERP systems, achieving first-data-live status in 11 weeks. The platform delivered consolidated spend by supplier, category, business unit, and period; duplicate supplier identification across all units; contract coverage analysis; and spend velocity tracking — the first time any of this information had been available at group level in the company's history. The immediate output was a savings map: $18.7M in addressable savings across three levers. Price harmonisation across business units managing the same supplier at different rates accounted for $6.2M. Contract compliance improvement — directing off-contract spend back to negotiated agreements — accounted for $7.8M. Category consolidation reducing supplier fragmentation in high-spend indirect categories accounted for $4.7M.

Pillar 2: Supplier Intelligence System

A supplier risk monitoring programme was implemented covering the top 400 suppliers by spend. Each supplier was scored monthly on financial health, operational risk, compliance status, and delivery performance trend. Within 60 days of go-live, two suppliers in the elevated-risk tier were identified for proactive management. One was replaced before a disruption occurred — the pattern of financial deterioration and delivery performance decline was consistent with the prior three emergency procurement events, and the early identification allowed a managed transition rather than a crisis response. The annualised supplier risk incident rate fell from three material incidents per year to one in the nine-month engagement period, a 67% reduction.

Pillar 3: Category Management Restructure

Fourteen business unit procurement teams were reorganised into seven consolidated category management teams with group-wide accountability. Category managers were redeployed from transactional processing to strategic activity: supplier relationship management, market intelligence, category strategy development, and commercial negotiation. Transactional processing was centralised into a shared procurement operations team handling PO generation, approval routing, and invoice matching for the full group. The shift in category manager time allocation from 10% strategic activity to 58% is where the compounding value of this transformation accrues over a multi-year horizon — it is the change that makes the Year 3 and Year 5 savings numbers meaningfully higher than Year 1.

Pillar 4: Technology Consolidation

Seven procurement systems were consolidated into a single group platform over nine months. E-procurement capability enabled supplier self-service for catalogued items, automated PO generation for repeat purchases, and electronic invoice processing — eliminating manual PO processing for 68% of transaction volume by month nine. The technology consolidation was the enabler of time reallocation, not the goal in itself. Every transformation programme that leads with the platform rather than the process redesign reproduces the same failure: the platform gets deployed, the process does not change, and the category managers remain trapped in transactional work.

Procurement transformation in multi-entity organisations is primarily a data and governance challenge, not a technology challenge. The platforms exist and work. The constraint is always the same: data integration across fragmented ERP environments, process standardisation across business units with different operating cultures, and the organisational willingness to consolidate fragmented teams behind group-level accountability. Companies that treat this as a technology project consistently underdeliver. Companies that treat it as a governance project, enabled by technology, consistently outperform their business cases — because the real savings are in the governance changes, not the platform features.

Key Takeaway

What This Engagement Demonstrates

The savings potential in a fragmented multi-entity procurement organisation is typically 2–3x larger than leadership estimates before a systematic spend analysis is conducted — because without consolidated data, the sources of value are invisible. Duplicate supplier pricing, off-contract spend leakage, and category consolidation opportunities all exist below the threshold of visibility that fragmented systems provide. The first investment in a procurement transformation is always the spend intelligence infrastructure that makes the savings case visible enough to fund the rest of the programme.

The ROI case typically shows payback within 4–6 months of savings realisation when disruption cost avoidance is included alongside efficiency savings. The disruption avoidance calculation — the expected value of the supply incidents that proactive supplier monitoring prevents — is frequently the margin between a business case that clears the investment hurdle and one that does not, because efficiency savings alone are real but insufficient to justify the organisational change that a genuine transformation requires.

The Broader Pattern

In multi-entity organisations grown through acquisition, 60–70% of procurement value is typically concentrated in 15–20% of the supplier base — and that supplier base is almost never managed at group level. A 10-week spend analysis will identify the addressable savings pool with sufficient precision to fund a full transformation programme. The analysis is the starting point most organisations skip in favour of a technology procurement — which is why most procurement technology investments in this context underdeliver.
