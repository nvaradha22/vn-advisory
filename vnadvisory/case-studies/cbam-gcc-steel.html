The Situation

An Export Business Facing a Structural Cost Shift

A mid-size integrated steel manufacturer in the GCC had built a significant export position in the EU market over 15 years. Their product mix — structural steel, rebar, and specialty sections — served European construction and infrastructure buyers. EU revenue represented 34% of total group turnover, making it the single largest revenue geography and the relationship most exposed to the structural shift that CBAM represented.

When CBAM entered full financial enforcement in 2026, the company's internal compliance team produced an annual liability estimate of €2.1M, based on Scope 1 direct emissions from the primary steelmaking process. This figure was used in initial board briefings and formed the basis of preliminary discussions with EU buyers about pricing adjustments. The company was managing the situation — or believed it was.

The actual liability, once a multi-tier carbon audit was completed, was €4.2M — exactly double the internal estimate. The gap came from three sources that the internal model had not captured:

  • A secondary furnace brought online 18 months earlier had not been incorporated into the Scope 1 emissions model, adding €680K in unaccounted direct liability.
  • The grid emissions factor for purchased electricity had been understated by 34%, a common error in markets where the grid mix changes more rapidly than reported emissions factors are updated.
  • Upstream Scope 3 carbon from imported iron ore pellets and alloy additions — required under CBAM's multi-tier accounting methodology — had not been captured in the model at all.

At projected 2028 ETS carbon prices of €95 per tonne, the restated liability would reach €6.8M annually without intervention. The gap between the board's understanding of CBAM exposure and the actual financial position was not a rounding error — it was a strategic problem that required immediate parallel action across energy, procurement, and compliance simultaneously.

Our Approach

Three-Track Intervention Across Nine Months

VN Advisory structured a parallel three-track response rather than a sequential one. The timeline to full CBAM enforcement did not allow for sequential deployment — each track needed to be advanced simultaneously, with the three workstreams coordinated to avoid creating compliance gaps between them.

Track 1: Energy Transition Acceleration

A solar project at feasibility stage had been stalled in the capital allocation review process for 14 months. The previous investment case had modelled the project as a sustainability investment with a 7.8-year payback — which placed it in a long capital queue competing against operational priorities. VN Advisory remodelled the investment case incorporating CBAM liability reduction as the primary financial return: the solar project would reduce annual CBAM costs by €1.1M at full generation capacity, producing a 4.2-year payback when the carbon cost avoidance was included in the model. The revised business case was approved at the next board capital committee meeting — the investment that had been blocked for 14 months cleared in a single cycle once the CBAM savings were on the table.

In parallel, a renewable energy purchasing agreement was executed with a regional renewable power provider, replacing 40% of grid electricity within six months. This delivered an immediate €420K annual Scope 2 liability reduction without the capital deployment timeline of the solar project — bridging the gap between the solar project's approval and its generation capacity coming online.

Track 2: Supplier Carbon Qualification

Four key raw material suppliers — iron ore pellets, ferroalloys, lime, and scrap — were assessed for carbon intensity using CBAM-compatible methodology. Two suppliers were replaced with lower-carbon alternatives at equivalent or lower procurement cost, demonstrating that the carbon transition in the supply base did not require accepting a price premium. One supplier was engaged in a joint carbon baseline programme that produced a 12% reduction in their declared carbon intensity through process improvement, reducing the embedded carbon in that input category and the associated certificate requirement for finished goods.

Track 3: Product-Level Carbon Declaration

The company had been filing CBAM declarations using a plant-average carbon intensity figure — treating all output as having the same emissions profile regardless of which production line or furnace produced it. Disaggregating to product-level declarations — differentiating the newer, more efficient furnace's output from the older line production — reduced certificate requirements for the lower-carbon product categories by 18%. This was a compliance accuracy improvement, not a physical change to production, but it had an immediate cash impact in reduced certificate purchase obligations for the affected product lines.

The critical intervention was not technical — it was commercial. Reframing the solar project from a "sustainability investment" competing in a crowded ESG capital queue to a "CBAM liability hedge with a defined 4.2-year payback" unlocked capital committee approval that had been blocked for over a year. The technology, the project, and the contractor were all ready. The constraint was the investment framing, not the investment.

Key Takeaway

What This Engagement Demonstrates

CBAM liability quantification is a multi-tier carbon accounting problem, not a sustainability reporting exercise. The gap between what internal compliance teams estimate and what a properly structured audit reveals is consistently 50–100% — because internal models are built for reporting, not for CBAM's specific multi-tier methodology covering Scope 1, Scope 2 grid factors, and upstream Scope 3 embedded carbon. Companies that have accepted their internal estimate as the basis for board decisions, pricing conversations with EU buyers, and capital planning are operating on a number that is likely half of their actual obligation.

The intervention lever with the highest immediate ROI is energy transition acceleration — not because renewable energy is the fastest intervention, but because the investment case for energy transition changes fundamentally when CBAM savings are included in the financial model. Projects that looked marginal at a standard discount rate show 3–4 year paybacks when the annual certificate cost reduction is on the table. The question to ask at every capital committee is: what does this project's payback look like with the CBAM savings included?

The Broader Pattern

Most GCC and Indian exporters with significant EU market exposure are managing their CBAM obligation based on an internal estimate that understates actual liability by 50–110%. The three most common sources of the gap are Scope 2 grid emissions factors, upstream Scope 3 raw material carbon, and production changes not reflected in the compliance model. A focused 6–8 week multi-tier audit is the starting point — not a multi-year sustainability programme.
