The Situation

A $680M Distributor Navigating Eight Countries on Spreadsheets

A regional FMCG distribution company operating across India, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, Qatar, and Egypt managed a network of 340 active suppliers, 12 distribution centres, and 4,200 retail accounts. Annual revenue was $680M. Supply chain management was conducted through a combination of ERP transaction recording, WhatsApp supplier communication, and manual tracking spreadsheets maintained by a planning team of 22 people — a system that had scaled with the business until the pace of disruption exceeded what manual tracking could absorb.

The planning team spent an estimated 65% of their time chasing status updates from suppliers and logistics providers rather than managing the supply chain. Only 28% of inbound shipments had real-time tracking data available in any system — the remainder required manual status requests with an average 4–6 hour response time per inquiry. The business was operating largely blind, reacting to information after the fact rather than sensing problems early enough to resolve them cheaply.

Exception identification was almost entirely reactive. The company typically discovered a supply problem when stock ran out or a retail customer complained. Average disruption identification-to-resolution time was 72 hours against an industry benchmark of 8–12 hours for comparable distribution networks. Emergency procurement spend — at a 25–40% premium to contracted prices — was running at $4.8M annually.

  • Stock-out incidents were running at 124 annualised — roughly one significant stock-out every three days across the network, each generating lost revenue and emergency logistics cost.
  • Without predictive visibility, the planning team could not distinguish high-priority exceptions from routine variance until the consequences were already in motion.
  • No exception was scored for financial impact on arrival — prioritisation was based on whoever escalated loudest rather than revenue-at-risk, producing systematic misallocation of planning attention.
Our Approach

Three-Phase Implementation Over 12 Months

VN Advisory designed a three-phase implementation sequenced to build each capability layer on the foundation of the previous one. Deploying AI exception management on fragmented, low-coverage data would have produced recommendations no planner could trust and no governance framework could safely automate. The sequence was a prerequisite, not a preference.

Phase 1: Data Integration Layer (Months 1–3)

A unified supply chain data platform was established integrating ERP inventory and demand data, carrier tracking APIs across three primary and twelve secondary carriers, supplier portal data via EDI and API for the top 60 suppliers covering 74% of spend, and retail customer POS data feeds from the top 200 accounts. The platform updated every 15 minutes — replacing the 6–24 hour lag of manual data collection entirely. Real-time supply chain visibility increased from 28% of inbound shipments to 89% within the first three months. That improvement alone — clean, current, network-wide data — was the foundation that made everything subsequent possible. Without it, the intelligence layer that followed would have had nothing reliable to operate on.

Phase 2: Exception Intelligence Engine (Months 3–7)

AI-powered exception detection and classification was deployed on the integrated data layer. The system learned from 18 months of historical data to identify signals that had preceded past disruptions — supplier lead time drift, carrier on-time delivery deterioration, inventory draw-down rate acceleration ahead of replenishment, POS demand acceleration ahead of the replenishment cycle. Each incoming exception was automatically scored for revenue-at-risk, recovery complexity, and time sensitivity — replacing 2–3 hours of daily senior planner triage with a prioritised queue that told planners what to address first and why. For each exception, the system generated 2–3 response options ranked by cost and lead time impact. Planner decision time per exception dropped from an average of 3.2 hours to 28 minutes.

Phase 3: Automated Resolution (Months 7–12)

A governance framework established three decision tiers. Tier 1 — automated without human intervention — covered carrier substitution within the approved panel below a defined cost threshold, buffer stock drawdown within defined limits, and standard replenishment acceleration for fast-moving SKUs with clear reorder triggers. Tier 2 — AI-recommended, human-approved — covered complex routing decisions and supplier escalations where relationship context mattered. Tier 3 — full planner discretion — covered high-stakes decisions, novel situations, and anything with consequences beyond defined parameters. By month 12, 41% of all supply exceptions were resolving at Tier 1 without human intervention. The planning team's exception management time had dropped from 65% of working hours to 28% — 8.1 person-years of capacity redirected to supplier development and category strategy.

The technology for supply chain decision intelligence is deployable at mid-market scale. The implementation constraint is data integration, not AI capability. Every organisation that has stalled in this type of transformation did so in Phase 1 — not because the AI failed, but because the data foundation was insufficient to support reliable recommendations. The organisations that invested the first three months in clean, unified, real-time data consistently achieved faster and higher ROI from the intelligence layer that followed. Those that rushed to the AI platform built on weak data learned the lesson expensively.

Key Takeaway

What This Engagement Demonstrates

The gap between reactive exception management and decision intelligence is not primarily a technology gap — it is a data and governance gap. The AI capability required to score exceptions, generate response options, and execute Tier 1 resolutions is available, proven, and accessible at mid-market price points. The constraint is the data integration and governance design that must precede it. Organisations that invest in the data foundation first, define the decision governance framework before deploying automation, and measure honestly at each phase consistently achieve the financial outcomes. Those that lead with the AI platform and build data and governance around it consistently underdeliver their business cases.

The 8.1 person-years of planning capacity redirected from exception chasing to supplier development is the transformation's least visible but most durable financial benefit. Expediting cost reduction and stock-out avoidance are measurable in year one. The compounding value of a planning team spending 72% of its time on strategic activity — rather than reactive exception processing — accumulates over three to five years in supplier relationship quality, category strategy execution, and supply base development that the previous operating model was structurally unable to prioritise.

The Broader Pattern

In distribution operations of this scale, emergency procurement premium spend typically runs at 0.5–1.5% of revenue — invisibly embedded in the procurement cost line and attributed to market conditions rather than exception management failure. A control tower deployment that reduces that premium by 35–45% and cuts stock-out incidents by two-thirds generates financial returns that dwarf the implementation cost in the first twelve months. The data foundation investment is the prerequisite that makes those returns achievable — and the step most organisations are tempted to shortcut.
