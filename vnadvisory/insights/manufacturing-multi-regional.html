Manufacturing Shift: From China+1 to Multi-Regional Networks

The China+1 strategy bought time. It did not buy resilience. Companies that moved to Vietnam, Bangladesh, or India as a single alternative to China have discovered that concentration risk does not disappear — it relocates. The next phase of manufacturing network design requires a different architecture.

THE PROBLEM

Why China+1 Has Already Been Competed Away

The China+1 strategy emerged from the COVID-era supply shock as a pragmatic response to single-country production concentration risk. Companies that had built their manufacturing around China recognised the fragility of that dependency and moved to establish an additional production location — typically Vietnam, India, Bangladesh, or Mexico — while maintaining China as the primary production base. The logic was sound. The execution, for the fastest movers, was well-timed. And the resilience advantage it created lasted roughly two years before the market neutralised it.

The fastest movers completed their China+1 transitions between 2021 and 2023. Their competitors followed. The resilience advantage of having a single alternative production location has now been arbitraged away by the market — because every company in the sector made the same move to the same geographies at the same time. Vietnam and Bangladesh in particular absorbed enormous manufacturing FDI in this period. Labour costs in those markets have risen, capacity is constrained, and the concentration risk that China+1 was designed to address has partially reconstituted itself in the alternative locations.

THE GCC–INDIA CORRIDOR OPPORTUNITY

For manufacturing and trading businesses with GCC market exposure and EU export ambitions, the GCC–India production corridor represents a strategically under-utilised opportunity. GCC capital, market access, and logistics infrastructure combined with Indian manufacturing capability, engineering skills, government PLI incentives, and cost position creates supply chain partnerships that serve both regional markets and EU export flows — with better CBAM positioning than Chinese or Southeast Asian alternatives. This corridor will become more strategically important as Western blocs tighten supply chain provenance requirements.

Three simultaneous shifts have made a single-alternative production location insufficient, regardless of which geography it is in. Tariff complexity has escalated beyond single-country risk: US-China tariffs, EU CBAM, and friend-shoring requirements mean production origin now affects the cost, regulatory exposure, and market access of the output — not just the supply risk. Regional market requirements are increasing: GCC government procurement, EU supply chain due diligence, and US domestic content requirements all create market access conditions tied to production geography. And climate regulation now creates production location effects: the carbon intensity of the energy grid at a manufacturing site affects the CBAM cost of EU-bound goods produced there. Low-carbon grid location is now a commercial input to manufacturing location decisions, not a sustainability metric.

WHAT IS CHANGING

Multi-Regional Networks Are the New Resilience Architecture

The shift from China+1 to multi-regional manufacturing is not merely geographic diversification. It is a different design philosophy: networks built to serve regional markets from regional production, with cross-network flexibility as a resilience layer — rather than a single global factory with a single backup. For companies with significant exposure across GCC, India, European, and North American markets, the emerging architecture has four production nodes, each defined by the market it primarily serves and the competitive advantage it offers in that market context.

Node Primary Market Competitive Advantage Key Regulatory Factor
GCC (UAE / KSA / Oman) GCC + African markets Logistics hub, energy cost, market access Localisation requirements for GCC procurement
India (Gujarat / Tamil Nadu / Maharashtra) India + EU export Labour cost, engineering skills, PLI incentives EU supply chain due diligence alignment
Southeast Asia (Vietnam / Indonesia) US + Asian markets Cost, US tariff positioning US friend-shoring, IRA requirements
Eastern Europe / Morocco EU markets CBAM advantage, EU FTA, proximity CBAM certificate reduction, EU regulatory alignment

The India node deserves particular emphasis. Government Production Linked Incentive schemes have made India materially more attractive as a manufacturing hub across 14 sectors since 2020. Incentive values range from 4–20% of incremental sales depending on sector, with the highest incentives in advanced chemistry cells, electronic components, pharmaceuticals, medical devices, and specialty steel. Combined with a large domestic market, a deep engineering and technical workforce, and improving port and logistics infrastructure — particularly in Gujarat and Tamil Nadu — the production environment available in India today is qualitatively different from what it was five years ago. Companies that assessed India as a manufacturing option in 2018 and decided against it should reassess.

THE IMPACT

What Multi-Regional Manufacturing Costs and Returns

The honest cost of multi-regional manufacturing versus concentrated single-site production is 8–18% higher total production cost, depending on product category, automation level, and overhead structure. That cost premium is real, should be modelled honestly, and should then be evaluated against four specific financial calculations that change the investment case.

Tariff avoidance is the most immediately quantifiable. Products manufactured in tariff-favoured locations avoid 25–145% tariff costs on relevant markets. For a $50M US-bound product line currently manufactured in China, the annual tariff exposure under current duties is $12.5–72.5M. The cost of establishing a production node in a tariff-favoured geography — even at a 15% unit cost premium — is a fraction of that exposure over any multi-year horizon.

Market access preservation is the second calculation, and it is growing in importance. GCC localisation requirements and EU supply chain due diligence are creating conditions where companies without the right production footprint are losing RFQ eligibility, not just commercial preference. Several GCC government tenders and major EU OEM procurement programmes now require in-region or in-country production as a qualification criterion. Companies without that footprint are not competing on price — they are not competing at all.

CBAM cost differential is the third. A production node on a low-carbon or renewable energy grid produces goods with significantly lower CBAM certificate requirements for EU export. The cost differential between a coal-grid and a renewable-grid aluminium production for EU export is 9–14% of product value at current ETS prices — and that differential widens as carbon prices rise toward the €95–120 per tonne trajectory that most market projections consider likely by 2028–2030.

Network design decisions have multi-year lead times and multi-decade consequences. The right time to redesign a manufacturing network is before competitive pressure forces it — when options are broader, capital is available on reasonable terms, and transitions can be sequenced rationally. Single-site concentration is not more efficient when tariff exposure, localisation risk, and business continuity requirements are properly priced into the total cost model. It is only cheaper in a cost model that excludes those variables.

WHAT TO DO

A Phased Network Transition

Multi-regional manufacturing is not a single capital project. It is a multi-year network transition that requires careful sequencing to manage capital deployment, operational complexity, and capability development in parallel. The organisations that have executed this transition most effectively did so in three phases, each building on the previous one rather than attempting simultaneous establishment of all nodes.

Phase 1, spanning the first 18 months, establishes assembly and finishing nodes in priority market geographies using local or regional contract manufacturing partners. This achieves production origin certification for market access requirements — GCC localisation, EU supply chain due diligence, US domestic content — with lower capital outlay and faster time-to-market than full greenfield manufacturing investment. The capital commitment is modest. The commercial benefit — restored RFQ eligibility, tariff reclassification, customer qualification compliance — is immediate. This is the phase where the financial case for the full transition gets proven in live commercial terms rather than modelled projections.

Phase 2, from month 18 to month 36, transfers core manufacturing processes to the production node that serves the market with the highest regulatory or commercial pressure for local production. This is the phase of largest capital commitment and operational complexity — building quality management systems, qualifying the local supplier base, transferring process knowledge, and running parallel production during the ramp period. The organisations that execute this phase most effectively invest heavily in capability transfer rather than just equipment transfer. A new factory with the right machines but without the process knowledge embedded in the workforce is not a manufacturing asset — it is an expensive building.

Phase 3, from year three onward, optimises production volume allocation across operating nodes based on real-time cost, logistics, regulatory, and carbon factors. This is the phase where AI-driven network optimisation creates ongoing value — continuously rebalancing production across nodes as tariff regimes shift, carbon prices change, logistics costs fluctuate, and market demand evolves. The network that was designed for resilience in Phase 1 and 2 becomes a genuine source of competitive advantage in Phase 3, because its flexibility is an asset that single-node competitors cannot replicate quickly.

CONCLUSION

Network Architecture Is the New Competitive Advantage

The competitive advantage in manufacturing is shifting from production cost optimisation to network architecture. The companies that have built multi-node, regionally responsive production networks will outcompete those still optimising a single global factory — on cost-adjusted terms when tariff and regulatory exposure is included, on market access when localisation requirements are enforced, and on resilience when the next supply disruption arrives, as it will.

The transition cost is real. An 8–18% unit cost premium for multi-regional production is a genuine commercial commitment. The question is not whether to pay a cost — it is which cost to pay. The cost of building a multi-regional network on your own timeline, sequenced rationally and funded from operating cash flow, is consistently lower than the cost of making the same transition under commercial duress — when tariff enforcement has already hit margins, when market access has already been restricted, and when competitors who moved earlier are already capturing the business you are scrambling to recover. That comparison, honestly modelled over a five-year horizon, is the business case for moving now.
